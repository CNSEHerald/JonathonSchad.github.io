Please Stop Using Rape as a Plot Device

 

 

By Jacqueline Elwood, Editor-in-Chief | 22 April 2016

Opinion

bustle.com

So you’ve written a book or a screenplay. Congratulations. You give it to your editor or your director so they can look it over and they have one complaint. They tell you, “this subplot doesn’t make sense with the natural flow of the rest of the story,” or “I don’t understand this character’s motivation for taking action,” or “what's this character’s backstory that makes her [or him] act this way.”

 

You wrack your brain, trying to figure out how to satisfy your editor or director’s needs. And then it hits you. It was so simple. You add in a scene, a scene that will make everything clear. You have the character in question raped. Now everything makes sense. Right? Hardly. Rape and sexual assault are too often used in media for a means to an end without delving into the emotional and societal tolls it creates. Without this representation, media only contributes to the fetishization and eroticism that pervades rape today.

 

Rape is a serious social issue that is often ignored. And yet some believe it’s a good way to add some “shock factor” or provide motivation for a specific character. That’s not what rape should be in media. But that does not stop writers from writing it in. They think it gives them ratings? Trust me, it doesn’t.

 

Season five of Game of Thrones is likely the most notorious example of using rape as a plot device. In episode six of GoT’s fifth season, writer Bryan Cogman (likely with permission of series writers David Benioff and D. B. Weiss) ended the episode by having fan-favorite Sansa Stark raped by her new husband, the odious Ramsey Bolton. Why? Well because fan-hated Theon Greyjoy was in the room watching. As Ramsey carries out the act, the camera pans to Theon as he watches and zooms in for a close up shot to reveal Theon’s coming redemption arc. The rape becomes a plot device to motivate Theon to eventually leave Winterfell with Ramsey’s bride in tow.

 

In an attempt to create a dramatic turning point for a male through the rape a female lead, GoT managed to anger their fan base. According to Rotten Tomatoes, the fifth season of the series had boasted around 100 percent positive reviews of all their episodes until episode six, the episode that ended with Sansa’s rape. The percentage of positive reviews tanked to below 60 percent after that episode aired and took a couple episodes to recover.

 

For some fans, GoT will never recover from that episode. Season five deviated from the books more than the previous seasons. And the treatment of Sansa’s arc was the most offensive. In the books, Ramsey never marries Sansa. They’re not even in the same area of Westeros. The writers of the GoT TV show consciously wrote in this rape for the plot driving element they believed it could achieve. And I, as an avid reader and fan of the series, refuse to watch the series again because of this.

 

The cringe worthy season premier of American Horror Story: Hotel featured a figure referred to as the Addiction Demon. In the first episode, one character, a heroine addict, is violently raped by the figure that leads to his death. American Horror Story has always been known for trying to push the boundaries, but this time they took it a little too far.

 

The purpose of the character is rather obscure. The show later explains that patrons of the hotel that would check in to do drugs created this “Addiction Demon”. So naturally, a demon that symbolizes addiction and was created by drug addicts would rape people. It makes perfect sense (You may sense my sarcasm). The only symbolic meaning of the demon relating it to its origins is its name. But its method of punishing individuals is used to add a shock factor and another brutal, rather traumatizing, way to kill characters of the show.

 

And these shows are only the tip of the proverbial iceberg. Rape has been used in many other TV shows such as Outlander, Veronica Mars, Mad Men, House of Cards, Downton Abbey, and Reign. Law & Order: SVU makes it the focus of their crime drama. Soap operas are notorious for using rape as a means of sudden plot advancement or to create more drama. And some sit-coms have made jokes about sexual assault for [cheap] comedic value.

 

I’m not arguing that instances of sexual violence should be completely cut from television. That would be trying to avoid the issue. Sexual violence is a serious problem in the United States and rape culture is something that needs to be addressed and combated. But using the suffering of a human being, albeit fictional, for the sole purpose of motivating a character or moving a plot along is a far cry from the kind of dialogue we need to create.

 

So all I ask is, unless you want to write about the effect sexual violence has on people or how victims have dealt with the after effects, don’t think about using rape. And never use it as a convenient plot device.

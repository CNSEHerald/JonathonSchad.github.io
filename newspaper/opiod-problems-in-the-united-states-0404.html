Op-ed: Don't Do Drugs

By Elizabeth Blackert

Opinions Editor

addiction.com

“I think the public doesn’t fully appreciate yet the scope of the problem,” President Obama stated at the National Rx Drug Abuse & Heroin Summit.  The problem in need of discussion is the ubiquitous misuse and abuse of opioids, prescribed and illicit.  While many college students may consider using drugs as an interesting experience, or even a joke, the danger that these drugs pose is more serious than most realize.

 

Opioids are prescribed as effective pain relievers.  While they do nothing to treat the cause of the pain, they work in the brain to reduce the intensity of pain signals.  This is part of the problem—being content with treating the symptom rather than the cause.  Almost everyone has consumed some kind of opioid, a category that includes hydrocodone, oxycodone, and morphine, at some point in their life.  While these drugs should be primarily used to alleviate pains due to surgeries, some doctors seem to hand them out like candy to silence patients’ complaints.  In addition, overuse and sharing of these too-easily obtained drugs occurs more often than not.

 

Who or what is to blame for the thousands of opioid related deaths that occur each year?  Is it the drug companies who downplay the side effects and addictive properties of the prescription drugs? Are they any different from the heroin dealers on the street?  Or is it the doctors who offer them as a solution for chronic pains, which only serves to add to the population of addicted individuals?  Is it really right to blame the people who are addicted to these drugs when there are so many ways to easily become so?  All of the involved parties need reform for this terrible problem to be solved.

 

The Obama administration recognized this need and began its quest to ease the opioid epidemic in 2010, with a National Drug Control Strategy (NDCS).  And yet, opioids have now surpassed car crashes as one of the leading causes of death in the US.  The NDCS was a noble effort, but the continued and dramatic rise in annual overdoses shows that it was not nearly enough.  Luckily, this is one of the few issues upon which political leaders agree—more needs to be done.  However, how to go about achieving the goal of public health is not so easily decided.

 

Obama’s 2017 budget plan includes $1.5 billion to go towards treatment and prevention strategies.  This seems promising, but simply throwing more money at an ever-increasing problem does not seem like a complete solution.  A fundamental overhaul of the system that allows drug companies to pay doctors for prescribing their medicine, that allows one in four people seeking treatment for chronic pain to become addicted, that doesn’t stop the thousands of addicts from using dirty needles to dose themselves with ever-increasing amounts until they finally overdose, is required.

 

Change is required, and change starts with you.  Stop making light of serious issues; drug abuse should not be socially acceptable.  Share your concerns and knowledge about this problem that affects millions of your fellow Americans.  Be aware that just one time can easily become much more.  Finally, just listen to the words that have been preached since middle school: don’t do drugs.

SUNY Poly Administration Comments on Middle States Accreditation

 

 

By Deanna Soucie | 1 April 2016

Following evaluation from the Middle States Commission for Higher Education (MSCHE) in February, the student body of CNSE has had questions concerning the suggestions and recommendations made by the MSCHE committee and the fate of SUNY Polytechnic Institute’s accreditation. To better understand what the results of this visit meant for the school and its students, the Herald met with the SUNY Polytechnic Institute Provost William Durgin, Interim Head of the Nanoscience Constellation at CNSE Alain Diebold, and Professor Kathleen Dunn who helped to write the self-study that was submitted to MSCHE.

 

MSCHE is a regional accreditation committee consisting of thirteen commission members which acts on the behalf of the Department of Education to assure the excellence of universities in Delaware, the District of Columbia, Maryland, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands.  As an institution of higher education, SUNY Poly periodically has to demonstrate that it meets the fourteen standards of excellence that the MSCHE puts forward.

 

SUNY Poly began preparations for the recent MSCHE visit some years ago, by “preparing what’s called a self-study,” explained Durgin. “Now in a self-study, the whole institution has to engage in determining how we personally feel as an institution that we have met the standards of excellence.”  After this study was completed, a self-study report was drafted. The authors were three SUNY Poly faculty members— Professor Kathleen Dunn, Vice President of Human Resources Rhonda Haines, and Dr. Daniel White.  This is the report that was presented to the MSCHE visiting committee that came to CNSE last month.

 

The visiting committee consisted of a group of eight representatives from other universities.  After receiving SUNY Poly’s self-study report, the committee held two days of meetings to investigate issues that were not made clear by the self-assessment.  According to Durgin, “They want to see that you have plans to make things better, and we did quite well. The team members came up with essentially the same list of things that we need to improve.”  This committee will compose a report which will be delivered to the MSCHE by the chair, Dr. John Anderson, in June or July.

 

When SUNY Poly’s case comes before the MSCHE in several months, Anderson “will present the report and make his recommendation to the board, the board will deliberate on that recommendation and then they will vote to decide what they are going to give us for accreditation,” stated Durgin. “I would say as an aside here, that there is absolutely no question that we will be reaccredited.”

 

Durgin explained that there are five different outcomes that can happen when the MSCHE hears SUNY Poly’s case.

 

“One is that you’ll simply be reaccredited for another ten years.  The next is that you’ll be reaccredited and they’ll make some recommendations that you fix this or fix that. The third would be that we would be reaccredited, but we’ve got some significant problems that we will have to fix and report back to them.  The fourth is that the problems they identified are relatively serious and there’s going to have to be a revisit.  The last would be not to reaccredit, that’s terribly serious and it happens very infrequently.”

 

Dunn went on to explain just how rare a case it is when a university’s accreditation is pulled. “They really have only given that when, for example, they can’t make payroll next year or your enrollments are unsustainable.”  In all other cases, universities get reaccredited while they work to fix problems identified by the MSCHE.

 

The visiting committee issued nonbinding recommendations and binding suggestions after their visit.  When the suggestions were released at the conference held by Anderson, the student body raised questions, particularly in the case of Standard 9, the section concerning student life.  The lack of dormitories and a health and wellness center are a concern among some of the students at CNSE, and it has made several students wonder why there were no binding suggestions made in this area.

 

Standard 9 was met because SUNY Poly recognized these shortcomings and has plans in place to fix them.  “They didn’t tell us we had to do anything because we told them that we had all of these plans in place and they were satisfied that we had plans,” says Durgin.  To clarify what typically results in a binding suggestion, Dunn followed by saying “it’s really bad when they find things that we didn’t recognize about ourselves.”

 

The visiting committee that was sent to SUNY Poly was comprised largely of members whose universities had gone through mergers, and they were sympathetic with how long it can take to “get the pieces in place”, as Dunn put it.  “One of the members happened to mention that his institution, which was a merged institution,” Durgin recounted, “it took ten years before they got everything pretty much well cleaned up.”

 

The MSCHE’s final decision will not be posted until July or August, but in the meantime, there are several changes around CNSE that students should start to see.  First, more self-assessments will be done and student surveys will be taken. SUNY Poly wants to determine if classes are appropriately preparing students for careers after undergraduate education. To do this, SUNY Poly intends to survey employers of CNSE alumni.

 

Dunn assures that before the current freshman graduate in 2019, students can expect to see many changes.  Dorms will be built, the health and wellness center will be put in place, distance learning classes will be developed, the diversity of general education classes and professors will be expanded, and we will have “more faculty in general, in the science and engineering areas as well. We are actively searching for new faculty so we can continue to grow.”

 

Kathy Dunn believes that "no university is perfect.  No one goes through [accreditation] and says ‘we have no problems’, getting accreditation doesn’t mean you’re perfect, what it means is you are aware of your problems and are working to fix them".  She and William Durgin are both confident that SUNY Poly will hear good news from the MSCHE over the summer.

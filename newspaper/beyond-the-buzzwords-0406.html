Op-ed: Beyond the Buzzwords of New York's Finest Work-in-Progress

 

 

By Malcolm Graves | 6 April 2016

If buzzwords were flowers, then CNSE is a prolific botanical garden, replete with daffodils, tulips, and daisies.  In other words, our college, the 80% industry and 20% academia establishment, is surrounded by words like “world-class”, “leading-edge”, and “ground-breaking.”  The official website, along with news releases, is filled with these buzzwords.  But do they reflect the true nature of Dr. Kaloyeros’ brainchild for undergraduates?  Or are there more accurate ways to describe it?

 

Let’s consider some facts first.  Over $24 billion has been invested in this facility, of which many companies are stationed, most of them being related to the semiconductor industry. There are over 4000 people employed here at CNSE.  Class sizes are fairly small.  The facility is very new compared to most technology colleges.  As a state school, it has lower tuition rates than private technology schools (e.g. RPI, RIT, etc.).  The world’s first 7 nm chip was built here.

 

At first glance, CNSE seems like quite a bargain to prospective undergraduate students.  The space-age facility stands out from the concrete and brick buildings of most universities.  The emphasis on the presence of shiny cleanrooms and stainless-steel equipment during the facility’s tours captivate naïve high schoolers.  And it is hard to forget the grandeur of the massive EUV lithography machine in NanoFab X.  The state-of-the-art nature of the facility is undisputed.  But how much of the tours and promotional rhetoric actually applies to students?  Investors and business people may be genuinely interested in the fancy machinery, but can the same be said for most students, who are simply looking for an affordable and meaningful education?  What is the point of highlighting these expensive resources if they are inaccessible to students for years to come?  The high-tech facade does amaze newcomers as if it was a tangible buzzword, but it hardly reflects on the undergraduate programs. When there are myriad options for STEM-oriented undergraduates, it is only reasonable to focus on resources available to them for their time here; to do otherwise is to mislead them.  Little information is given about academic aspects more relevant to students.  Indeed, the tours and promotions give a spurious impression that the fancy technology, most of which is exclusive to industry, somehow equates to amazing, unparalleled educational opportunities.

 

Maybe the buzzwords and technology hide some undesirable traits of an education here at CNSE in an attempt to mask the overwhelming industry focus.  For instance, student life here pales in comparison to many other state schools; there are less than ten active clubs, a student lounge has only just been created, and no dedicated dorms even exist yet.  There is almost nothing to do outside of academic work, creating an atmosphere that is almost as sterile as the cleanrooms themselves.  It is up to the students to fill this void of organized social life.  Compared to most universities, this is a major weakness; such extracurriculars are often highlights of the college experience.  In addition, the curriculum is relatively restricted; there are only two majors (Nanoscale Engineering or Nanoscale Science) with four different concentrations.  Some of our departments (e.g. biology) are notably underdeveloped compared to other universities.  Most of the industry is isolated from academia as far as undergraduates are concerned, save for some networking events from a small handful of companies.  On the administrative side, students have dealt with various delays, lack of transparency (i.e. effective communication with the student body), and unfulfilled promises.  Aside from a few electives and cross registering with UAlbany courses, there are few options to diversify one’s schedule.  Then again, what do you expect from a small college dedicated to nanotechnology?

 

There is one aspect of CNSE that may live up to its buzzwords, however.  That is education.  While it is debatable whether the education here is comparable to that of MIT or Stanford (which was stated in a Faculty Meet & Greet session), it is definitely not run-of-the-mill.  CNSE may not have the cutthroat acceptance rates, but that does not mean it is a walk in the park either.  The curriculum is challenging and unique, attributes that most students here can concur with in later years.  In addition, the labs and exposure to industry is noteworthy at the very least.  Not many other STEM majors can use a scanning electron microscope, perform photolithography, fabricate nanorods, or create hydrogels so early in their curriculum, nor can they access professors and labs as easily.  Where else could you find quality introductory classes of less than 50 students, or personally interact with professors performing advanced research in your freshman year?  In fields like science and engineering, close relationships with both peers and professors are invaluable, and to develop them as early as freshman year is quite advantageous.  Establishing a network is, in the long run, easy with dozens of companies on-site, especially in later years.  And past graduates have gone to schools such as the University of Michigan, Stanford, MIT, and UC Berkeley for their graduate degrees, further indicating the quality of education at CNSE.  For a state school price, it is definitely worth the money at the very least, provided you put the work and take the initiative.

 

Public relations (official website, pamphlets, etc.) should make the aforementioned details more explicit when promoting or describing the college.  While flowery buzzwords are not unique to college websites, CNSE is quite the bouquet.  These words though are vague and give the impression the curriculum lacks substance.  More concrete details more accurately describe the program, and should be stated upfront.  The accessibility of professors and labs should be explicitly stated.  Small class sizes should be noted.  The types of procedures performed in lab courses should be hinted at.  Myriad examples of successful graduates should be provided.  These will impress prospective students or other inquirers more than “groundbreaking” and “truly innovative.”  Students can always back up these statements (though make sure they give details too).  If writing detailed college essays gets students admitted, providing detailed information gets applications submitted.  Plus, doesn’t it seem rational to better reflect on the program and acknowledge the hard work the staff has done to accommodate undergraduates?

 

CNSE, of course, is far from perfect.  Dorms and organized social life are still far away.  Students are just beginning to get the acknowledgement they deserve from the administration.  Maybe the buzzwords will return – and with better reason this time – if the undergraduate programs become better known, and when we actually have a full complement of student facilities.  For now, let us make sure students know, in detail, what CNSE has to offer them.

 

You may now wonder, do the buzzwords reflect the benefits CNSE provides to the New York economy?  Is it worth the taxpayer’s money?  An intriguing question – for another time.

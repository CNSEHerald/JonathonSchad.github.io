NY Legislature Enacts SUNY-wide In-state Tuition Freeze for Next Fiscal Year

 

 

By Audrey Crom | 15 April 2016

Life & Local

SUNY students have been holding demonstrations in favor of tuition freeze

Democrat & Chronicle

This past week, the New York State Legislature and Governor Andrew Cuomo finalized the New York state budget and failed to renew the NY SUNY 2020 legislation, which had previously allowed for a steady, predictable increase in SUNY tuition.

 

The NY SUNY 2020 legislation was signed by Gov. Cuomo in 2011, which authorized all SUNY and CUNY campuses to raise tuition annually by $300 and allowed for University Centers at Albany, Stony Brook, Binghamton, and Buffalo to annually increase out-of-state tuition by 10% as well.  This bill became effective on July 1, 2011 and is set to expire on July 1, 2016, five years after its implementation.

 

According to the official statement at suny.edu, the purpose of the NY SUNY 2020 Grant Challenge was to "elevate SUNY as a catalyst for regional economic development and affordable education".  This essentially resulted in families being able to readily and predictably prepare for any tuition changes for the first time in the history of SUNY.

 

 Prior to this bill, SUNY students were faced with dramatic and unpredictable tuition hikes, averaging around an increase in 6.7% annually, and often climbing upwards of 40%, which is well above the proposed flat rate increase of $300. These large increases were largely due to a decline in state support on SUNY costs, shrinking from about 60% to 36% in the last 10 years and leaving students to cover the remaining cost with rising tuition prices.

 

The failure of the SUNY 2020 bill to achieve renewal translates to a SUNY and CUNY wide freeze for in-state tuition while still enacting the 10% increase for out-of-state students for this upcoming fiscal year.  On the surface, this tuition freeze is good news for the average in-state college student; however, this leads to less money available for SUNY institutions to allocate towards resources, research, and other campus services.

 

SUNY had originally proposed this tuition freeze for the 2016-2017 fiscal year in exchange for a $78 million increase in funding provided by the state in order to cover projected mandatory costs, mentioning that further tuition increases were unnecessary if there was an increase in support shown from the state.Â  However, due to budget negotiations and available funds, this amount was reduced down to $18 million, leaving a considerable deficit to account for which many SUNY campuses fear they will not be able to meet.

 

 Due to the fact that the number of in-state students far outweighs that of out-of-state students, the 10% tuition increase from out-of-state students is projected to bring in an extra $22 million, falling short of the necessary $60 million.  Despite this discrepancy, the tuition-freeze still went into effect.

 

The rejection of the SUNY 2020 bill and the in-state tuition freeze only applies to the 2016-2017 fiscal year, which begins on July 1st, 2017 and ends on June 30th, 2017.  There is a possibility that sporadic and unpredictable tuition hikes may resume for the 2017-2018 fiscal year after the tuition freeze is lifted.  The SUNY 2020 bill is slated to be up for negotiation once again during the next budget proposal for the 2017-2018 fiscal year.

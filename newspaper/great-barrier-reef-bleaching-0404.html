The Great Barrier Reef Turns White

By Ana Segarceanu

Relnews.com

Tourists traveling to Australia to see the Great Barrier Reef may be surprised by the white coral and the absence of fish covering the sea floor.  No longer colorful, the coral populations are experiencing a rise in bleaching.

 

Zooxanthellae, a species of marine algae, live inside the tissue of different coral species.  This alga helps the coral produce food and at least 90% of their energy.  The coral, when stressed, expels the tiny photosynthetic Zooxanthellae.  If the alga cannot repopulate the coral, then the coral dies.  Without this symbiotic relationship, the corals’ tissues become transparent and their white skeletons are revealed.  This process is known as bleaching.  When bleaching occurs, the coral lose their ability to produce food and energy, begin to starve, and eventually die.

 

Bleaching occurs when nature patterns, pollution, or rising sea temperatures externally stress the coral populations.  Strong waves and hurricanes can break apart and degrade colonies, having devastating effects on the slow growing coral.  Rising temperatures and an El Niño weather pattern can cause thermal stress, massive bleaching, and affect fertilization.  A study by the National Marine Fisheries Services Office of Protected Resources in 2001 has shown that during 1997’s El Niño season, 70% to 80% of the coral in the Pacific and Caribbean areas were killed due to bleaching.

 

Climate change and rising temperatures have been the main reason for the most recent occurrences of bleaching.  Scientists have stated that this has been the worst bleaching in history, with most damages to the coral reefs being categorized as severe.  The reefs closest to the Queensland shore and coral populations from Cape Flattery to Mackay have experienced the highest levels of bleaching.

 

Coral reefs are a valuable part of marine habitats.  Healthy reefs help control the carbon dioxide concentration in water, prevent erosion by protecting shorelines from wave action, provide shelter and protection to many species increasing diversity, and even help the local economies.  According to NOAA, National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, in 2001, the value of fisheries from coral reefs in the U.S. was over $100 million.  Coral reefs provide a quarter of the fish imported into developing countries.  The decrease of coral in reefs all around the world can have lasting detrimental effects.

 

Steps have been taken to protect coral reefs around the world.  In 1998, the U.S. created the Coral Reef Task Force to conserve coral reefs.  They are responsible for mapping and monitoring U.S. coral reefs, research the causes and solutions to coral degradation and death, and carry out strategies to sustain and conserve coral reefs.  Many other countries have similar programs.  The Australian Research Council Centre of Excellence for Coral Reef Studies, based in Australia, researches ways to sustain and manage coral reefs.  Scientists hope that with a global effort to improve water quality, decrease pollution, and through fishing management, coral reefs can be maintained and have a better chance of survival.

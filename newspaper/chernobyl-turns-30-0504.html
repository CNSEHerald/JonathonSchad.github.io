Chernobyl Disaster Celebrates 30th Birthday

 

 

By Jacqueline Elwood | 04 May 2016

World News

Town of Pripyat in Ukraine 30 years after the explosion at the Chernobyl power plant

First to Know

April 26th marked the 30th anniversary of the disaster at the Chernobyl nuclear power plant in Ukraine. Atomic material was spewed into the air, poisoning a swath of land about the size of Luxembourg.

 

The power plant sits inside an 18-mile exclusion zone. The meltdown in Prypyat resulted in the death of 31 workers and firemen, mostly from acute radiation sickness. In the three decades since, thousands more have died from radiation-related illnesses, including cancer.

 

Despite the millions of people that were forced to evacuate, some opted to remain within the exclusion zone refusing to leave the home they were born in.

 

Environmental nonprofit Greenpeace conducted a report revealing that people living in the affected areas are still coming in daily contact with dangerously high levels of radiation.

 

“Due to the reduced financial support to deal with the aftermath of the Chernobyl disaster, control of contaminated foodstuffs is reduced, less money is available to implement protective measures, and less scientific monitoring data are available,” Greenpeace said in the report.

 

The Chernobyl disaster emitted large quantities of caesium-137 and iodine-131. According to some estimates, the disaster released 5,300 PetaBecquerels of radiation – nearly 10 times more than what was released in the Fukushima disaster in 2011.

 

Given the half-life of Cs-137 is 30 years it will take several centuries for the radioactive pollution to decay.

 

The wildlife in the area, however, has recovered in the midst of radiated land. The animals in the exclusion zone may have shorter lifespans and produce less offspring. Denys Vyshnevskiy, a biologist in Chernobyl’s exclusion zone, told AFP during a visit that the number and variety of species have been growing at rates unseen since long before the Soviet Union’s collapse.

 

“Radiation is always here and it has its negative impact,” said Vyshnevskiy. “But it is not as significant as the absence of human intervention.”

 

Ukraine was not the only country trying to recover from the disaster. When the Chernobyl disaster occurred, an estimated 70 percent of the fall-out fell on Belarus, a country to the north of Ukraine. The radiation caused nearly 9,000 cancer deaths and parts of the country are uninhabitable.

 

Before Chernobyl, Belarus relies on Ukraine for their energy supply. Currently, the country pays about $800 million annually to Russia for natural gas shipments. Now, they plan to reduce their reliance on Russia by building a new nuclear reactor of their own. The reactor has been in the planning stages since 2007. The first reactor will be commissioned in Nov. 2018 and the second in July 2020.

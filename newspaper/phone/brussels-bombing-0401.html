Devastation in Brussels

 

By Jacqueline Elwood | 1 April 2016

Brussels airport in Zaventem damaged by two suicide bombers early on the morning of March 22nd

Bloomberg

On March 22nd, the citizens of Brussels became the victims of an ISIS attack that left 32 people dead and 300 more injured. This is the second major terrorist attack in Europe within the last year. Investigations are currently ongoing as to what transpired and who was involved in the Tuesday morning attack.

 

Early in the morning on March 22nd, two suicide bombers, identified later to be Najim Laachraoui and Ibrahim El Bakraoui, attacked the departure lounge of the Brussels Airport in Zaventem.  The attack resulted in the death of at least 10 people. There was a third bomb left at the airport by another suspect.  This bomb detonated because of unstable explosives. No one was injured since security forces had already secured the scene.

 

About an hour after the attacks at the airport, Khalid El Bakraoui, the brother of Ibrahim El Bakraoui, detonated a suicide bomb in the second car of a train at the Maelbeek metro station.

 

The identity of the third suspect in the airport attacks is still under investigation. Belgian authorities arrested Fayçal Cheffou on March 24th under suspicion that he was the third airport attacker.  Cheffou was released Monday due to lack of evidence. In a telephone interview, Eric Van der Sijpt, a spokesman for the prosecutor’s office, explained the release of Cheffou.

 

“We’re not saying that he’s innocent. That we do not do. It’s that he’s no longer needed in prison.  But there are two different things. Preventative custody has nothing to do with the actual investigations or the charges brought.”

 

Mounting unrest has been building in Brussels following the attacks. Evidence that the authorities ignored or misinterpreted signs that could have prevented the attacks has spawned public outrage. Several unverified reports have surfaced that warned Belgian authorities in advance of the attacks planned against the Brussels airport.

 

According to Skai TV in Greece, a map and layout of the Brussels airport were found in January 2015.  The map was found on a computer and USB drive in a flat in Athens. The Paris attacks ringleader, Abdelhamid Abaaoud, had occupied the flat.

 

French daily Le Monde reported handwritten notes and attack plans found on the Athens computer hard drive. They noted that the details matched the bombing of the Brussels airport.

 

U.S. intelligence officials stated that they were not surprised that Brussels was attacked.  One U.S. counterterrorism official even stated, “The Belgians have been sitting on a ticking time bomb.”

 

Counterterrorism officials have focused on Belgium for years. Large numbers of Belgian foreign fighters have left to join ISIS and other terrorist groups in Syria and Iraq – more per capita than any other country in the European Union.

 

Thomas Joscelyn, senior fellow at the Foundation for Defense of Democracies and senior editor of The Long War Journal, commented on Fox News about the attacks.

 

“If you talk to any Belgian counterterrorism or intelligence officials, no one – no one – is surprised by this. You can go all the way back to 2014 to Belgian authorities saying this was coming. They’ve launched a series of counterterrorism raids because they know that known terrorists are operating on their soil.  This is the least surprising outcome that you could see.”

 

The Belgian authorities are hard at work trying to piece together the events from that morning.  The Brussels airport is still closed but hopes to partially open the airport soon. The Brussles Airport has been sending out updates through their Twitter feed on the status of reopening the airport.

 

Arnaud Feist, the director of the Brussels airport, stated that temporary facilities are being put in place.  These would be designed to process about 800 travelers per hour, only about 20 percent of normal operation.  According to Feist, it could take months before the airport is fully operational again.

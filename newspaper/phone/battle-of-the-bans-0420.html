Battle of the Bans

 

By Deanna Soucie | 20 April 2016

Cumberland News Now

In the past month, North Carolina and Mississippi passed legislation legalizing forms of LGBT discrimination.  In response to the infringement of LGBT rights, some companies, states, cities, and individuals have banned or boycotted nonessential interaction with North Carolina and Mississippi.

 

In late March, North Carolina passed House Bill 1523, which bans transgender individuals from using restrooms which do not match their biological sex at birth, and bars municipalities from creating their own antidiscrimination policies.  According to the NY Times, the bill also prohibits local governments from raising minimum wage levels above the state level.  In early April, Mississippi passed a similar law allowing businesses to refuse service to LGBT individuals based on religious objections.

 

Following each law allowing discriminatory practices, many entities spoke out against these states.  Governor Andrew Cuomo (D-N.Y.) banned all nonessential state travel to North Carolina and Mississippi.  Governor Cuomo issued a statement regarding the law passed in Mississippi.

 

“Discrimination is not a New York value. We believe our diversity is our greatest strength, and we will continue to reject the politics of division and exclusion.  This Mississippi law is a sad, hateful injustice against the LGBT community, and I will not allow any non-essential official travel to that state until it is repealed.”

 

This is not the first time states have been boycotted over ‘religious liberty’ laws.  Indiana was the target of a New York travel ban in 2015 after it passed an LGBT discriminatory law, which has since been repealed.

 

Other states, including Vermont and Washington, and cities, such as Portland, San Francisco, Seattle, and New York City, have passed similar travel bans to these states.

 

Musicians Bruce Springsteen and Bryan Adams cancelled shows they had planned in North Carolina and Mississippi, respectively.

 

Major pornographic website XHampster.com has made its content no longer visible to computers with a North Carolina IP address.  It will soon display a petition against the new bill for users in that area.  XHampster went on to release popular search terms for the state, emphasizing those that suggest homosexual or transgender content.  The term ‘transsexual’ was searched 400,000 times in March and ‘gay’ was searched 320,000 times.  XHampster spokesperson Mike Kulich hopes that their petition will get as many signatures as there are ‘transsexual’ searches.

 

North Carolina and Mississippi are not just facing travel and entertainment bans, but industry slowdowns as well.  PayPal had plans to invest $3.6 million global operations center in Charlotte, N.C. that would create 400 new jobs.  This plan has since been cancelled because the new law “violates the values and principles that are at the core of PayPal’s mission and culture” said president and CEO of PayPal, Dan Shulman.

 

PayPal was not the only company to voice its disapproval.  According to the Portland Press Herald, chief executives of 80 companies including Facebook, Apple, and Google have urged North Carolina to repeal the law.

 

Additionally, this is not the only recent attack on Mississippi over discriminatory laws.  A law enacted in 2000 that prohibited same-sex couples from adopting children was recently repealed in the wake of the 2015 Supreme Court marriage ruling that gave all same-sex couples in the United States the right to marriage.

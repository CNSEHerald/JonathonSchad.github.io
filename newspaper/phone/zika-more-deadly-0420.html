Zika Found to be more Deadly than Originally Thought

 

By Audrey Crom | 20 April 2016

CDC

Recent studies of the Zika virus are inciting doubt amongst experts on whether mosquitoes serve as the primary vector for infection and spread of the disease to humans. The University of California Los Angeles (UCLA) partnered with the Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences and Peking Union Medical College in Beijing where researchers conducted a study surveying the sequence of genes exhibited by Zika in the past decades and found a display of incongruent sequences between human and mosquito strains.

 

Researchers analyzed and compared 40 strains of the virus from past outbreaks as well as the current epidemic. Strains were collected from humans, monkeys, and mosquitoes, but none of the strains found within humans have been matched with those found in mosquitoes. This indicates that more people than initially expected contracted the virus by other means than through mosquitoes. The researchers elaborate that other methods of transmission, such as sex, may prove to play an even larger role than previously believed. The Zika virus marks the first time a virus originally transmitted through mosquitoes has been shown to spread through other means.

 

The UCLA research group found several notable changes in the viral sequence over the past decades. They suggest that the virus’ ability to mutate has allowed it to trigger different types of diseases within hosts, such as microcephaly, Guillian-Barre syndrome, various other autoimmune diseases, and paralysis. These mutations may allow the virus to divide more rapidly and evade the immune system entirely. This display of rapid mutation helps explain why the virus had previously not been linked to the cause of diseases before the recent outbreak of 2015. Before then, the Zika virus was deemed relatively benign and found mainly in mosquitoes.

 

 Earlier this month, the Obama Administration allocated the remaining funds originally dedicated to fight the spread of Ebola in West Africa towards Zika research and prevention effort after a nearly two month deadlock on funding with Congress. A total of $589 million will be donated to research in containing Zika, where $510 million of which would be taken directly from the Ebola budget. The remaining $79 million is to come from several other outlets, such as funds dedicated towards the strategic stockpile of vaccines and other emergency supplies for epidemics.

 

These funds, however, are not enough to effectively and fully support an all-encompassing effort against the Zika outbreak. $589 million is a fraction of the $1.8 billion of emergency funds requested by the Obama administration to Congress back in February. Congress responded by suggesting that the remaining funds allotted for Ebola be spent first, effectively turning down the administration’s $1.8 billion request, at least temporarily.

 

 The research team at UCLA plans on carrying out further investigation on the current strain of Zika in order to isolate and identify any areas of interest or targets that would aid in the development and administration of potential drugs or vaccines. Although the United States does not currently exhibit any “hot zones” of Zika activity, the virus is spreading steadily through Puerto Rico.

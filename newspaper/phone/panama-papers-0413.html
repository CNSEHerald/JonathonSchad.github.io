Who's Who in the Panama Papers

 

By Jacqueline Elwood | 13 April 2016

Terabytes of data leaked from Panamanian law firm Mossack Fonesca

The New Yorker

The Panama Papers is the largest data leak to date.  The German newspaper Süddeutsche Zeitung obtained around 2.6 terabytes of information leaked from the world’s fourth largest law firm, Mossack Fonesca.

 

The source of the files has opted to remain anonymous, only speaking with the German newspaper through encrypted channels.  The newspaper shared the files with the International Consortium of Investigative Journalism (ICIJ) where journalists have begun sorting through the mass of information.

 

The leaked files contain information on more than 214,000 offshore entities connected to people in more than 200 countries and territories.  With revelations from the leak and their repercussions still unfolding, more and more political figures may be implicated in the future.  Some political and organization leaders have already come under fire as a result of the initial investigations.

 

Mossack Fonesca

 

Mossack Fonesca is a Panama-based law firm that administers offshore firms to people and businesses. While the firm is based in Panama, it has a global network with 600 people working in 42 countries. Some of these countries include the tax havens of Switzerland, Cyprus, the British Virgin Islands, and Seychelles.

 

Many of Mossack Fonseca’s clients do not seem to be using their companies for improper purposes. The documents indicate, however, that the firm’s customers have included Ponzi schemers, drug kingpins, tax evaders, and at least one jailed sex offender. Some companies through Mossack Fonesca have even been linked to financing organizations or countries that pose a threat to other countries’ national security.

 

The company has since published a response to the allegations in the Guardian.

 

“We would like to take this opportunity to clarify that we have never knowingly allowed the use of our companies by individuals having any relationship with North Korea, Zimbabwe, Syria, and other countries mentioned by [the Guardian] that might have been considered as a threat to any other country’s national security or that have been listed by a sanctioning body.”

 

Sigmundur Gunnlaugsson

 

Iceland’s Prime Minister Sigmundur Gunnlaugsson stepped down on April 4th after the documents revealed an offshore company in his wife’s name. Gunnlaugsson purportedly held close to $4 million in Icelandic bank bonds through a company in the British Virgin Islands.  He allegedly sold this company to his wife for $1 before becoming Prime Minister in 2009 while his country was in the middle of a financial crisis.

 

Gusslaugsson claimed that his resignation would be temporary. The Agriculture and Fisheries Minister Sigurdur Ingi Johannsson replaced Gusslaugsson on April 5th.  Gusslaugsson released a statement that Johannsson is only an “interim” leader.

 

Sergei Roldugin and Vladmir Putin

 

Cellist Sergei Roldugin, a close friend of Russian President Vladmir Putin, has been linked to a company behind $2 billion in tax-free funds. Putin has denied any involvement in offshore financial activities in light of Roldugin’s involvement. The Russian president slammed the Panama Papers as a conspiracy by the West aimed at weakening Russia’s global standing.

 

The news bulletin of Russia’s Channel 5, a national network, began the morning news with an announcement about Putin with regards to the Panama Papers.  “Western journalists have begun their latest attempt to attack Russia with information.”  The news anchor informed the viewers that, “They have not presented a single document that would directly implicate the President.  It’s all hints and assumptions, as we’ve seen more than once before.”

 

Putin has not been explicitly named in any of the documents uncovered.

 

David Cameron

 

U.K. leader David Cameron has received criticism after admitting he benefited from an offshore trust fund set up by his late father. He told the Guardian that he sold his shares in the company shortly before becoming Prime Minister in 2010.

 

Chinese Officials

 

Eight members of the Politiburo Standing Committee – the top body of the communist Chinese government – have been implicated in the Panama Papers. All mentions of the leak, however, have been censored from the Internet. The brother-in-law of Chinese President Xi Jinping has been mentioned in the list of offshore beneficiaries.

 

FIFA

 

FIFA ethics committee member Juan Pedro Damiani resigned from his position on April 5th. Damiani was linked to three men convicted as a result of a recent a corruption investigation into FIFA. Swiss police raided European football association UEFA as well.  The Panama Papers indicated the current FIFA president, Gianni Infantino, had co-signed a 2006 broadcast deal with two men accused of offering bribes.

 

Transparency International

 

Founded in 1993, Transparency International is an international global organization based in Berlin, Germany. This global civil organization aims to fight to end corruption across the world. The president of the foundation’s Chile branch, Gonzalo Delaveau, resigned after being linked to at least five offshore accounts.

 

Many more have been implicated in the Panama Papers and information about them can be found on the ICIJ’s website.

 

The ICIJ will release the full list of companies and people linked to offshore accounts from Mossack Fonesca in early May.

New Student Lounge Creates Mixed Responses

 

By Erin Rousseau | 15 April 2016

The new SUNY Poly Albany site student lounge before it was opened up to students on March 31st

sunycnse.com

The long awaited SUNY Poly Student Space is open at the Albany site.  After years of emails regarding the unprofessional nature of sitting in halls, undergraduate students finally have a place to study and relax in between classes.

 

On March 31st, students and faculty waited outside the door, ready to access the student space.  They were greeted by a large open room lined with many smaller meeting spaces.  Sparse, professional furniture was used to functionalize the area.  For the event, a small ribbon was strung across two polls in front of a podium where the President and CEO of the college was to speak.

 

Though the ribbon cutting ceremony was scheduled for 2PM, the ceremony did not get underway until closer to 3PM.  Representatives from the Graduate Students Association and Undergraduate Student Association flanked President Kaloyeros, equipped with comically sized ribbon cutting shears.

 

SUNY Poly’s press release regarding the student center touted the space as being:

 

“The student center includes two lounge areas boasting approximately 20,000 square feet and is capable of accommodating more than 150 students at a time. Several dozen couches and coffee tables create a comfortable space for relaxation and social interaction... High-speed Internet service is available throughout the space, which also includes a fully equipped kitchenette.”

 

Students have raised questions regarding the legitimacy of this press release.  The kitchenettes contain a total of two sinks, a dishwasher, and three fridges between the two areas — a far cry from “fully equipped”.  Several dozen couches may be a stretch as well.  There are three full couches in the current lounge and ten if one counts the individual sections.  Even so, students are happy to have a place to work and are generally looking forward to the furnishings to come in the future.

 

One student remarked that, “I think it is a pretty nice space. Maybe it is kind of empty, surprisingly.  I thought there’d be a lot more furniture and desks and stuff.  [There is] a little more air volume than I expected.”  Michael Murphy, a graduate student who also attended CNSE as an undergraduate, stated, “I think it’s overdue and it looks better than expected.”

A student lounge is not a new concept at SUNY Poly.  In fall of 2010, there was a dedicated student lounge.  It was appropriated in the spring of 2011 and the students were given a table in the graduate offices.  When the graduate offices were moved to their current location, that access was stripped, leaving students without any dedicated space at the Albany site.

 

After the opening of the student space, the undergraduate and graduate class alike was sent a mass email detailing defacement of the area.  Zack Wheeler wrote that furniture in the space was not to be rearranged or moved in any way and that markers had been used to deface the furniture.  It was added that students were given a privilege to access the area where “cameras have been activated and security will monitor the rooms”.

 

Upon investigation it was revealed that the markers used in the defacement were dry erase and the surfaces written on were cubical dividers.  These dividers are comprised of a dry erase white board material, which allowed the stain to be removed without causing permanent damage.

 

The graduate student population was included in the original message as they are allowed access into the undergraduate space.  Some students expressed an initial apprehension about allowing undergraduates into graduate space after reports of defacement, though word spread quickly to assuage these concerns.

 

Murphy had assumed the entire ordeal had been blown out of proportion, and was never concerned about the maturity of the undergraduate class.  “Too many people here are worried about new space being vandalized.  They aren’t actually looking at the facts before they make their opinions. ”

 

Even without the ability to move furniture, the Undergraduate Student Association still hopes to make the lounge customizable.  The current set-up is only temporary.  Sofas and chairs without wheels may be moved to make the space more usable depending on student input.

 

Recent talks with the designer have shed some light on big plans for the student space in the future.  But for now, the Undergraduate Student Association plans to move into their offices in the student space by the end of the year.  Clubs may be able to secure a spot by the end of academic year as well.

Censorship and Free Speech Violations Occur Over ASP’s Sexual Assault Article

 

 

By Deanna Soucie| 29 April 2016

Life & Local

The University at Albany Lecture Center hallways were the site of a recent censorship scandal

Erin Rousseau

The Albany Student Press promotes the story of censorship and violations of free speech that were aimed at their paper in mid-April 2016.

In mid-April, Albany Student Press (ASP) ran a story titled “Assault Reports Up 200 Percent at UAlbany” to discuss how growing awareness of sexual assault resources led to a rise in reported assults. The article explains how increased reporting benefits the university. However, a UAlbany tour guide urged other guides to recycle all of the papers in the lecture centers to prevent prospective students from seeing the headline during the accepted student weekend.

 

According to the National Sexual Violence Resource Center, 1 in 5 women and 1 in 16 men are sexually assaulted while attending college.  With approximately 17,000 undergraduate students at UAlbany and a nearly equal male to female ratio, it is estimated that 1,700 women and 530 men are assaulted at UAlbany each year. Despite the projected high number of assaults, few are actually reported. In 2014, only about 75 assaults were reported, but this number rose to 150 in 2015, a 200% increase.

 

Chantelle Cleary, UAlbany’s Title IX Coordinator, told the ASP that she is happy about the rise in reports because it means that awareness of sexual assault resources has increased among the student body.  This is only a start though, as roughly only 7% of assaults are being reported. Cleary hopes to see these numbers continue to grow.

 

The ASP ran this story earlier this month to bring awareness to sexual assault, the need to report it, the new services offered, and how students have complete control on whether an investigation will be conducted.  When a UAlbany tour guide saw the headline in the lecture centers prior to the Accepted Student Open House, they made the decision to orchestrate the removal of ASP papers from the campus to cover up this story from visiting families.

According to the ASP, the following message was sent from the unknown tour guide to the tour guides’ group chat:   “Hi team!  If anyone is in the LCs today and sees the ASP that says something about assault.  Please remove them.  Grab a stack and recycle them.  I felt horrible doing it but it’s just not something we want to welcome our families with this weekend.” Despite feeling ‘horrible’ about the decision, this tour guide, and others censored ASP’s papers out of view.

 

The university was quick to respond and criticize the actions taken by the guide.  Provost James Stellar said the guide made “an unfortunate decision to remove copies of the Albany Student Press from certain distribution points across campus in an effort to hide from visitors the contents of an article on campus sexual assault”.

 

The ASP was also quick to respond, their paper has since featured two stories addressing this issue. “Student Newspapers Hidden During Accepted Student Open House”, brought this issue to light, shared the group chat that ASP obtained, and included the Provost’s comments. Additionally, the editorial “We Were Censored” was featured in [last week’s] publication. The article in which the editors decried the actions taken against their paper, while thanking the university for their aid.

 

The editors of the ASP called this a violation of their rights of free speech, rights that have never been taken from them in their 100 years of operation.  The editors spoke directly to the unknown tour guide who instigated this act. To this individual they said, “Your actions were shameful and ignorant…College is a place where ideas are supposed to be encouraged and celebrated, not squashed”.  The editors stated that this person was operating under the assumption that their actions would help the school, and called this “absurd and incorrect”.

 

The ASP followed this by addressing the university, saying they hope this employee faces consequences for their actions and that the members of the newspaper appreciate how quickly the university addressed this issue.  They concluded by telling the “person responsible for this… if you continue to remain a member of this university community, we, as the place where everyone on campus can have their voice heard, would welcome a story from you explaining your action”.

 

For more information on this subject, please visit the Albany Student Press website.

About the SUNY Polytechnic Institute's College of Arts and Sciences

 

 

By Deanna Soucie | 1 April 2016

Life & Local

sunypoly.edu

While attending a university, students are faced with a multitude of class options.  Until recently, SUNY Poly did not offer the wide breath of classes usually associated with higher education.  Courses like ‘introduction to film’ and ‘history of jazz’ are absent from the Course Catalogue.  These classes provide students with curricula outside of their majors, which can yield fun and relaxing experiences.  Currently at SUNY Poly, this multifaceted education is missing.  The problem is not that the courses are absent, but that word has not spread regarding their arrival.

 

In the Spring of 2015, prospective humanities and mathematics professors were asked to give demonstration lectures at the Albany site.  In addition to being an effective means of interviewing professors, the practice classes spread awareness about general education courses for SUNY Poly students.  However, the underclassmen of CNSE, whom these new courses are intended for, could not attend these mock lectures, as many students were not yet enrolled.

 

The dearth of information regarding the College of Arts and Sciences and its courses has left the student body relatively unaware of its existence.  The first classes given by the four professors of the newly formed Albany branch of the SUNY Poly College of Arts and Sciences began in the Fall of 2015.  With no online course descriptions, the faculty distributed paper copies and sent out emails to the student body with information on these classes.  Due to the lack of student awareness, course enrollment has thus far been low.  Professor Katherine Evans, Head of the College of Arts and Science at the Albany campus, teaches two classes this semester; one of which has three students, while the other class only has one.

 

Humanities courses taught this semester cover a broad range of topics and include the following:

  •  
  • Introduction to Literature: Banned Books in the Digital Age
  • Topics in American Literature: Graphic Novels
  • Introduction to Philosophy
  • Technology and Ethics
  • Arts and Culture: Native American Art and Culture
  • Understanding Human Nature

 

These are the only courses taught at the Albany site, but CNSE students have the option to take online classes or spend a semester at the Utica campus.

 

Evans believes that it is possible for SUNY Poly students to earn a minor in mathematics “through course offerings ... through directed studies, or online courses, there would be a way to work out the requirements for that minor without Albany students having to go to Utica.”  CNSE students could also pursue other minors with accommodations from Utica.  Students have the option of doing a semester of study at the Utica campus to take advantage of courses offered there.

 

The courses offered by the College of Arts and Sciences provide students with more opportunities for enrichment than simply filling general education requirements.  Evans believes that “part of the value of what we bring is that we teach courses that are so different from what students get in their nano bio, science, and engineering majors.”  Courses offered through the College of Arts and Sciences deal with topics pertaining to technology, ethics, human values and subjects that diverge completely from the field of science.  Evans says the College of Arts and Sciences faculty are always thinking about how to tailor classes for the students.

 

“‘What I study and what I research and know about and teach, how can I make that relevant to students here?’  But we also know that we aren’t scientists, and that’s not what our value is. Our value is to give students a broader perspective and to challenge them outside of their discipline.”

 

For students interested in learning about classes that will be offered in the Fall 2016 semester, a list of course descriptions will be compiled by early April and disseminated to the students via email.

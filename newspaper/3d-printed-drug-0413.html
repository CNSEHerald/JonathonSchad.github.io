FDA Approves First 3D Printed Drug

 

 

By Elizabeth Blackert | 13 April 2016

Science & Technology

Vos iz Neias

In August of 2015, for the first time, the FDA approved a 3D-printed drug called Spirtam.  As of March 22, 2016, the drug is now available in the US.  Spritam, produced by Aprecia Pharmaceuticals, is a prescription pill that will aid in the treatment of various forms of epilepsy, a condition that affects around 3 million Americans.  Perhaps equally important, it is a drug created through a process that may set a precedent revolutionizing the pharmaceutical industry.

 

All pills are composed of complex combinations of active ingredients that make them effective treatments as well as inactive ingredients that bind and make them suitable for ingestion.  Manufacturing the highly specific formulations with consistency is a challenge that pharmaceutical companies have faced as long as the industry has existed.  Compression machines, while now highly automated and far more efficient, have been in use since 1843.  The method by which the final product is obtained has not been updated significantly in over 150 years — until now.

 

Aprecia Pharmaceuticals’ 3D printing process works by layering powdered medications with an aqueous fluid that binds the mixture.  It can reliably produce pills with more specific and previously unobtainable properties by assembling the elements layer-by-layer at a microscopic level.  For example, the physical composition of pills can be made more porous than those produced by the compression method, allowing for pills such as Spritam to dissolve almost instantly with a single sip of water.  This is an important development for patients who require high doses of medication but have difficulty swallowing them, a common situation for sufferers of epilepsy. 3D printers can also produce pills with the utmost precision.  Whereas most current manufacturers divide pills from larger batches that are unlikely to have a completely even distribution of medication, designers of 3D printed drugs have unparalleled control over every aspect of the medication.

 

The process is also a great leap forward in the direction of personalized medicine.  Currently, factories produce pills in large batches that would require significant work if any changes in composition were desired.  3D-printers, on the other hand, could adapt easily to changes with a simple uploaded command.  Speed and strength of the dosage delivery are important characteristics that can easily be adjusted based on composition using the 3D printing method.  By varying these and other details, doctors could create patient-specific treatments based on the patient’s specific form of disease, medical history, genome, and even personal preferences for features like flavor.  Patients who used to take a myriad of drugs could now have their own “cure-all” pill printed for them, reducing costs and effort.

 

The FDA’s approval of Spritam and its 3D printing assembly technique helps demonstrate the method’s potential.  It is a lengthy and costly process to get drugs approved by the FDA; to have made it through this careful screening process with such a novel concept shows that it has merit.  Further evidence of the method’s perceived promise is found in the $35 million in financing that Aprecia Pharmaceuticals now possesses.  While it may be some time before this fabrication method reaches its full potential beyond the production of Spritam, it is evident that it has helped to initiate a paradigm shift in the way the world views pill production.

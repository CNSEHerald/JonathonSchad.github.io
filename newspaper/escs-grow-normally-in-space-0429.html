ESCs Grow Normally in Space

 

 

By Audrey Crom | 29 April 2016

Science & Technology

Image of an embryonic stem cell

Popular Science

 This past week, scientists from China were able to successfully cultivate and grow mammalian embryos in microgravity conditions within a satellite orbiting Earth, marking the first time that such cells were able to be developed in space.

 

The satellite carried 6,000 mice embryos within the self-sufficient enclosure, and their growth was monitored through a time lapse of high-resolution images taken every few hours. The rate of growth of these cells in orbit corresponded with the time it takes for normal embryonic growth on Earth, reaching the blastocyst stage at 72 hours of development. This study indicates that it is theoretically possible for other mammals, such as humans, to someday give birth in space.

 

 Previous studies on embryonic stem cells (ESCs) conducted in simulated microgravity environments have suggested the ability for faster cell growth. Abba Zubair, M.D., Ph.D. from the Mayo Clinic Jacksonville, Florida wishes to expound on these previous studies and cultivate human ESCs within a bioreactor on the International Space Station. This is part of his proposed three step plan.

 

 This plan involves first investigating whether mesenchymal stem cells (MSC) and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSC) do grow better in space. The next step would be to return to space and grow enough stem cells to support an initial clinical trial within patients suffering from recent hemorrhagic stroke. The final stage is to explore the possibility for larger scale tissue, and possibly organ, growth on board the ISS in order to be used for human application and integration. The advantage of growing stem cells in space would give rise to faster proliferation and cultivation of these cells.

 

 Zubair wishes to focus on the treatment of hemorrhagic stroke victims to use these rapidly grown stem cells to help facilitate neurons and blood vessels in these patients. For a typical bone marrow transplant, however, a person could require anywhere from 200 to 500 million cells. Conventionally grown stem cells take roughly a month to fully develop enough for use, but the previous micro-gravity ESC growth trials suggest that growth is increased five to ten times their normal rate, which would effectively cut down the time it takes for therapeutic stem cell generation by a significant amount. There are currently no viable treatment options available for patient use that would allow for doctors to help regenerate brain tissue. If ESC’s were able to be grown quickly and in large batches, this may help resolve the issues surrounding this issue.

 

 In current applications MSC’s are used in stimulating cell regeneration and tissue repair within the body. These cells also hold the potential for inducing complex organ regeneration, such as in the heart, kidney and joints. This study plans to investigate the prospect of integrating these space grown MSC’s safely into the human body in the same capacity that Earth-grown MSC’s are utilized. If such efforts prove to be successful, this may open new doors on to whether working systems, or even complex life, can develop within the zero-gravity conditions of space.  The science-fiction concept of non-planetary birth now seems within the realm of possibility, but whether or not the human race ever achieves such a prospect in the near future is another debate entirely.

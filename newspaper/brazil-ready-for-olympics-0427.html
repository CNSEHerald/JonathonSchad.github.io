Is Brazil ready for the 2016 Olympics?

 

 

By Elizabeth Blackert | 27 April 2016

World News

Quartz News

With a possible presidential impeachment, evidently unreliable infrastructure, and sanitation concerns, it is no wonder that some are beginning to question Brazil’s readiness for the 2016 Summer Games.  Brazil was awarded the games in 2009 when it was a growing world power, but the country has since destabilized.

 

Political turmoil has caused much of the destabilization.  President Dilma Rousseff was accused of funding various welfare and other government programs through use of dubious accounting tricks, breaking multiple budgetary laws in the process to cover the deficits before the 2014 elections.  On Sunday, April 17, the lower house of Brazil’s congress voted 367 to 137 in favor of Rousseff’s impeachment.  This came after a parliamentary commission cleared her of wrongdoing in October of last year.

 

President Rousseff, whose constituents overwhelmingly rated her government as “bad or terrible” in a recent poll, denied any wrong-doing and likened the accusations against her to a “coup.”  She stated that it will have “serious consequences for the Brazilian political process.”  Vice President Michel Temer, Speaker of the Lower House Eduardo Cunha, head of the Upper House Renan Calheiros, (all three in succession to the Presidency) and a multitude of other high-level Brazilian government officials who are in favor of President Rousseff’s impeachment were also accused of corruption.

 

Another vote will be held on May 17 in the Senate to determine if an impeachment trial will be held.  If the Senate votes yes, Vice President Temer will preside during the trial process and, as the process can take up to 180 days, may be the one holding the torch at the commencement of the Olympic Games.

 

President Rousseff’s purported actions have been linked to Brazil’s recent economic troubles—the country’s worst recession in recent history.  Its economy has been shrinking, housing spending and business sentiment are dangerously low, and inflation is widespread.  The uncertainty surrounding the country’s leadership status has led to market activity tumultuously driven by speculation.  The financial market has taken a slight upturn in recent months, possibly due to the prospect of Rousseff’s ejection from office.  The operating budget, however, for the Olympic Games was still scaled down at the beginning of the year from $1.9 to $1.85 billion.

 

The shrink in budget comes as the list of necessary preparations for the games grows.  Just last week, an elevated bike path in Rio de Janeiro collapsed under a wave, killing at least two people.  The path, which runs along one of Rio’s most popular beaches, was completed in January as part of the “Olympic City” initiative.  A local civil engineer attributed the tragedy to a “design flaw,” and stated that the structural integrity of the remaining sections of paths will be assessed.  Another engineering risk management expert called for the inspection of all Olympic Games-related structures.  It was noted before in a Brazilian paper that Brazil seems to have a “problem with trying to hurry projects to meet deadlines.”

 

In addition to these crises, Brazil also faces health safety concerns stemming from two sources: sanitation and the Zika virus.  Fourteen rivers flow through and around Rio into Guanabara Bay, and each one of them is filled with garbage.  Each year, hundreds of thousands of pounds of unprocessed waste reach the bay and nearby waters, which are the planned locations for multiple Olympic events.  A test in 2015 showed that the water was prolific with viruses and bacteria from human sewage, with visible trash and rotting fish abound in the more stagnant areas.  Mosquitoes here are rampant, and therefore so is the Zika virus.  This places Brazil on the list of countries to which pregnant women should not travel due to the link between Zika and birth defects.  These issues are major concerns for Olympic Athletes, whose success in the games depends on their maintained health.

 

Even with all the problems facing the host country, the International Olympic Committee (ICO) and various Brazilian officials remain optimistic.  They claim the political situation will not have a large impact this late in the planning stages of the games, and that the inflation will provide an attractive exchange rate to foreigners.  President of the Rio 2016 organizing committee maintains that the city will be ready, despite the delays in facility development and poor ticket sales, attributing the latter to “Brazilians’ love to buy tickets at the last moment.”

 

Regardless of Brazil’s situation, the Olympic Torch was lit in Athens on Thursday, April 21.  The torch will travel to Brazil and embark on a 95 day tour around the country before reaching Rio.  The President of the ICO gave a speech at the torch-lighting ceremony stating, “In these difficult days that Brazil is facing, the flame is a timeless reminder that we all are part of the same humanity.”  If all goes well, the torch will arrive at Rio’s Maracana Stadium where all will be prepared for the Olympic Games’ Opening Ceremony on August 5.

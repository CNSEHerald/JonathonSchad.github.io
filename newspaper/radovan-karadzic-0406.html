Radovan Karadzic's Trial: Shining Hope for Human Rights or Another Example of Incompetence?

 

 

By Jacqueline Elwood | 6 April 2016

On March 24th, former president of Republika Srpska, Radovan Karadzic, was found guilty of genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity.  The U.N.-established International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia (ICTY) sentenced 70-year-old Karadzic to 40 years in prison.  This sentence comes almost 21 years after he was indicted for genocide and crimes against humanity in July of 1995.

 

Karadzic was convicted for his involvement in detaining thousands of Bosnian Muslims and Bosnian Croats in prison facilities, orchestrating the mass killings in Srebrenica (which resulted in the death of 8,000 Bosnian Muslims), murder of civilians during the 44-month siege of the Bosnian capital, Sarajevo, and accompanying bombardment and sniper fire aimed at city residents.

 

The ex-Serbian leader was acquitted of one count of genocide for crimes committed in municipalities in eastern Bosnia, the Sarajevo region, and the Autonomous Region of Krajina.

 

Karadzic’s sentence has been praised by U.N. officials as historic.  Prosecutor Serge Brammertz made a statement that the verdict and sentence “will stand against continuing attempts at denying the suffering of thousands and the crimes committed in the former Yugoslavia.”

 

“Moments like this should also remind us that in innumerable conflicts around the world today, millions of victims are now waiting for their own justice,” he added. “This judgment shows that it is possible to deliver it.”

 

This judgment, however, was not seen as a victory from other groups.  Families of the victims believe that Karadzic’s punishment was not severe enough.  They complain that Karadzic could conceivably be released on good behavior in less than 20 years since he has already served about eight years while he was on trial.

 

Bosnian journalist, Nidzara Ahmetasevic, wrote to Aljazeera about the verdict.  Ahmetasevic believes that the implications of Karadzic’s sentence were not accessible to the people of Bosnia.

 

“After 23 years of work, the international tribunal in The Hague did not succeed in having a real impact on the people of the region. We did not hear loudly and clearly the judgment against nationalism, even though the judges did issue many.”

 

Ahmetasevic also believes that the decision was esoteric to the Bosnian people because the “official language used by the institution is not one of the regional languages; personnel by large do not come from the region; [and] the law they operate under has nothing to do with regional laws.”

 

Ahmetasevic was not the only person to criticize the decision.  The Serbian government made a statement that was read out by Justice Minister Nikola Selakovic after a special cabinet meeting dedicated to the verdict.  “The Serbian government believes that the justice of punishing members of only one nation for the crimes which were committed by all is in fact selective.” He added that all sides committed atrocities during the wars in Yugoslavia in the 1990s.

 

The Serbian tabloid Informer published a poster of Karadzic in their March 26th edition.  The poster shows Karadzic in uniform with the slogan “Serbia remembers.”  This is likely in reference to the slight Serbians feel regarding the one-sided trial.

 

The head of the Serbian Radical Party, Vojislav Seselj, protested against the EU and NATO at a rally of about 5,000 protesters, and said the verdict on Karadzic was “a sentence against the entire Serbian nation.”

 

With differing opinions about the 40-year sentence of Karadzic, it is difficult to predict how this decision will influence future war crimes tribunals.  Perhaps this was a step in the right direction.  The set-up of the ICTY and the sentencing of Karadzic only took around 21 years from the perpetration of the events in 1995.

 

The Extraordinary Chambers in the Courts of Cambodia (ECCC), more commonly referred to as the Khmer Rouge Tribunal, was established to try members of the Khmer Rouge who committed atrocities against the Cambodian people between 1975 and 1979.  The ECCC was not established until 2001 and did not receive official support from the U.N. until 2003.  This came 24 years after the Khmer Rouge was overthrown in Cambodia.

 

It took even longer for a major figure within the Khmer Rouge to be sentenced for the crimes against humanity committed between 1975 and 1979.  Kaing Guek Eav (alias Duch), a high-ranking commander under Pol Pot, was not given a sentence by the ECCC until 2010, 31 years after the fall of the Khmer Rouge.

 

The Permanent People’s Tribunal, which tried the atrocities committed by the Ottoman Empire against the Armenian people during WWI, did not reach a decision on whether or not this was considered genocide until 1984.  This comes 64 years after the acts were committed.  The genocide remains a hot-button issue for the Turkish government, which refuses to acknowledge the crimes committed by the Ottoman Empire, even to this day.

 

So it begs the question.  Was the sentencing of Karadzic a symbol of hope for the future of war crimes tribunals, or was it another example of inefficiency to try perpetrators of these crimes?

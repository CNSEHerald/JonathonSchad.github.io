First Nations Community in Canada Declares State of Emergency

 

 

By Brian Kelner | 22 April 2016

World News

People of the Attawapiskat tribe mourn the loss of members from the recent strain of suicides

NBC News

The Canadian First Nations community of Attawapiskat is in a self declared state of emergency after 11 people attempted suicide in a single night earlier this month. This small northeastern Ontario town of 1,500 people has seen over 100 suicide attempts since last September.

 

Attawapiskat is an extreme case, yet still representative of what life is like for the other 616 First Nations communities in Canada, where the suicide rates are 11 times that of the national average. Health Canada spokesman Sean Upton believes this stems from the long-standing social problems including overcrowding, bullying, addiction, the failing residential schools and systemic poverty.

 

Malcolm Gladwell discussed social effects that may contribute to suicides becoming such an epidemic in his book “The Tipping Point.” The act of committing suicide seems to be ingrained in the culture of the indigenous people as a ritual of adolescence. Suicide attempts are now so commonly seen that the seriousness and likely finality of the act is potentially trivialized in the minds of those considering taking their lives. This may provide insights into the plight of the First Nations’ people.

 

In the span of 20 years, Micronesia went from a group of small South Pacific islands where suicide was virtually unknown, to leading the world in attempted suicides per capita. A group of British sociologists studied this unique case. Their insights are paraphrased below.

 

Patients who attempt suicide were drawn from a section of the community in which self aggression was generally recognized as a means of conveying a certain kind of information. For these people, the act was viewed as comprehensible and consistent with the rest of the cultural pattern. If true, it followed that the individual who in situations of distress, wished to convey information about his or her difficulties to others did not  have to invent a communicational medium de novo.

 

The individual within this subculture could perform an act which carried preformed meaning; all he had to do was invoke it. The process is essentially similar to that whereby a person uses a word in the spoken language. Therefore suicides usually occur in clusters as the act of one person doing it gives an unspoken cue to others that the act is acceptable.

 

Hopefully the First Nations have these insights in mind as they are work in conjunction with the Canadian government to craft a  solution to this crisis. Perry Bellegarde, First Nations Assembly National Chief, recently called for a national strategy to address First Nations suicide. On Monday he tweeted "Immediate support to communities is essential & a #FirstNations driven national strategy on mental health”

 

Minister of Indigenous Affairs Carolyn Bennett has developed a two phase plan to combat this issue. As an immediate stop gap, more than a dozen crisis counsellors and two mental health counselors have been sent to Attawapiskat. They will stay there indefinitely, to leave only when the situation deescalates. Phase two includes building a community youth and additional housing, as well as designing youth-focused programming.

 

While these measures may not seem earth shattering, they have been effective in other communities. For example, Snuneymuxw has been suicide-free for five years, since their completion of a recreational center and implementation of youth programs focused on reviving traditional Snuneymuxw culture. Prior to this, their attempted suicide rate was well above the national average.

 

University of Victoria psychologist Christopher Lalonde claims to have data to support these claims. "The solutions to youth suicide in Attawapiskat or any other community are not going to come from Ottawa," he says. "They're going to come from communities taking ownership.”

 

Lalonde has monitored youth suicide rates in 196 First Nations for 21 years. His data suggests suicide is not a universal epidemic amongst First Nations. Rather, a tiny fraction of communities bear the heaviest risk. Amongst First Nations in British Columbia, more than 90 percent of suicides occur in only about 10 percent of communities. He has devised a system to mathematically define cultural health. The major link between the communities with high suicide rates was that they were the least culturally healthy.
